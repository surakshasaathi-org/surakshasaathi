Executive Summary

SurakshaSaathi is positioned as India's first full-spectrum protection companion — not a policy comparison portal, but a platform that serves policyholders before, during, and after insurance events. The name itself signals the brand promise: a trusted companion in protection, not a sales aggregator.

This document defines 8 product opportunities across three strategic clusters:

Cluster A — Claims & Recovery (Ideas 1, 8): Post-purchase advocacy for people who bought insurance but cannot use it.

Cluster B — Discovery & Advisory (Ideas 2, 3, 4, 5): Helping people understand what they have, what they're entitled to, and what gaps exist.

Cluster C — New Segments (Ideas 6, 7): Reaching MSME owners and senior citizens who are structurally underserved by existing platforms.

All 8 ideas are non-commodity and are designed to be structurally defensible against Policybazaar and Bima Sugam. The platform's moat compounds through proprietary data: rejection patterns, scheme eligibility databases, mis-selling signal libraries, and MSME risk templates.

The Market Gap in One Number

₹26,000 crore in health insurance claims were rejected or disallowed in FY24 — a 19% rise year-on-year. 11% of all claims are denied. 70% of PM-JAY eligible families are aware of the scheme but only 16% are enrolled. ₹14,000 crore in insurance money sits unclaimed. These are not edge cases. They are the mainstream Indian insurance experience.

SurakshaSaathi is built for the 500 million Indians on the wrong side of these numbers.

Idea 1  Claims Advocacy Portal

"India's insurance crisis isn't about buying — it's about using"

The Problem

₹26,000 crore in health insurance claims were rejected or disallowed in FY24 — a 19% year-on-year rise. The official rejection rate sits at 11%, but a consumer survey tells a starker story: only 25% of policyholders had their claim fully approved without friction. 33% received partial approval with questionable reasons. 36% faced outright rejection.

The Ombudsman system is powerful but opaque. 70% of Ombudsman cases overturn rejections — yet most people never file. Insurance Samadhan has operated in this space for 7 years, raised $5M, and generates ₹6 crore annual revenue on a manual model. That ceiling proves the TAM is real. The question is whether a tech-first approach can 10x their trajectory.

The Product

A three-layer AI-powered claims co-pilot:

Layer 1 — Post-rejection Fight Engine (launch)

Rejection Classifier: ingests rejection letter, maps against IRDAI guidelines and pattern database, outputs probability of reversal and fastest escalation path

Document Checklist Generator: based on rejection type, outputs exact document set for grievance cell → Ombudsman → consumer court

Escalation Letter Drafter: generates insurer grievance letter, IRDAI Bima Bharosa complaint, and Ombudsman filing in legally precise language

Case Tracker: monitors IRDAI 14-day resolution mandate, auto-escalates on deadline breach, alerts user on each status change

Layer 2 — Pre-Claim Readiness Check (Month 3)

User uploads policy. Engine maps waiting periods against disclosed conditions, flags exclusion risks, scores documentation completeness.

Outputs a 'Claim Readiness Score' (0–100) with specific actions to improve it before hospitalization.

Top rejection causes this addresses: waiting periods (25%), exclusions (25%), unanswered queries (18%), documentation errors (16%)

Layer 3 — B2B API (Month 9+)

HR/Corporate dashboard: aggregate claim health for group policy members, rejection rate trends, documentation gap alerts — priced per employee/year

Insurance broker white-label: proactive 'claim readiness report' for brokers to give clients post-sale

Hospital TPA liaison tool: helps billing departments structure discharge summaries to avoid auto-decline triggers under IRDAI March 2026 rules

Working capital note: Ombudsman cases take 3–4 months to resolve. The ₹499 registration fee and expedited track (₹1,999) partially bridge this gap. At 50 cases/month with 70% win rate and ₹13% fee: ₹5.5L MRR by Month 3.

Competitive Moat

Rejection pattern database: every case resolved adds a structured data point — insurer × ICD code × rejection reason × document that won. This compounds. Case 5,000 is cheaper to resolve than Case 1, unlike Insurance Samadhan's manual model.

IRDAI tailwind: Draft Ombudsman Rules 2025 propose 100% penalty on awarded amount for non-compliance. Makes escalation more credible, increases leverage.

Brand moat: the moment of claim rejection is peak emotional distress. A brand that shows up there owns the relationship.

Structural: Bima Sugam is prohibited from storing customer data, so cannot build longitudinal claim advocacy.

Risks & Mitigations

Working capital lock: mitigated via upfront registration fee and expedited paid track

Adverse selection — complex, old cases: mitigate by restricting launch to claims under 6 months old, amounts under ₹5 lakh, health insurance only

Insurance Samadhan copies product: their technical weakness is the answer; build the data layer they don't have

Regulatory: operates in gray zone as 'information and drafting service' not legal representation. Insurance Samadhan has operated 7 years in the same zone.

Idea 2  Policy Health Score

"Every aggregator optimizes for the sale. Nobody owns the 3 years after."

The Problem

India's insurance protection gap is growing at 4% per annum. The primary cause is not lack of purchase — it's lack of post-purchase management. People buy a policy, file it away, and forget it. Life changes (marriage, childbirth, parent's illness, salary jump, property purchase) that should trigger coverage reviews never do.

Bima Sugam is structurally prohibited from storing customer data — by regulation — which means it cannot do longitudinal advisory. That gap is permanent, and it is exactly where this product lives.

4% per annum (PwC India)

Policies bought and forgotten

Majority of retail policyholders per surveys

Bima Sugam data storage

Prohibited by regulation

Renewal commissions (broker)

12–18% first year, 5–7% renewal

Target customer

Urban dual-income family, ₹10–40L household income

The Product

A continuous 'CIBIL for insurance adequacy' — the Policy Health Score.

Users link all family policies (health, life, auto, term) to SurakshaSaathi — manually upload, or pull via Bima Sugam APIs once available.

The system computes a Policy Health Score (0–100) across five dimensions: coverage adequacy, redundancy/overlap, nominee accuracy, renewal risk, and gap alerts.

Score is recalculated on life triggers: add a family member, declare income change, receive a diagnosis, buy property, change job.

Gap alerts are actionable: 'Your father is 72 and not on your floater. He now qualifies for PM-JAY automatically — here's how to add him.'

Premium tier unlocks full advisory, claim support, and top-up policy recommendations (broker-assisted).

The score is the hook. The renewal and gap-fill commissions are the revenue.

Revenue Model

Renewal commission (broker)

White-label B2B (HR tools)

₹500–1,500/employee/year SaaS

₹2–5/API call for score data

Competitive Moat

Longitudinal data moat: every trigger event, every score change, every action taken builds a proprietary dataset of insurance lifecycle patterns. No aggregator has this.

Bima Sugam cannot compete: they are a transactional marketplace by regulation, not a relationship product.

Switching cost: once a family's entire portfolio is on the platform, moving is friction-heavy. Renewal reminders and auto-escalation create stickiness.

Risks & Mitigations

Adoption friction: getting people to upload all policies is hard. Mitigate with a 'scan your policy PDF' feature and eventual Bima Sugam API pull.

Monetization patience: revenue comes from renewals and placements, not upfront fees. Needs 90–120 days to first meaningful revenue.

Trust: users are sharing sensitive financial documents. Data security and DPDP Act compliance are non-negotiable from Day 1.

"The government built the safety net. Nobody built the door."

The problem is not awareness — it's navigation. People have heard of Ayushman Bharat. They don't know if they qualify, how to get the card, which hospitals accept it, or what to do when a hospital refuses it. The government's Jan Suraksha portal and 14555 helpline exist but are not consumer-grade products.

The Product

Function 1 — Eligibility Check (2 minutes)

User inputs: state, household income, age, Aadhaar linkage, occupation category

Output: full entitlement stack — central + state schemes, exact coverage amounts, what's covered vs. not, whether they already have an active card

Language: Hindi, Kannada, Tamil, Telugu, Bengali from launch. English optional.

Function 2 — Plain Language Explanation

Every scheme translated into 'what this means when you go to hospital': which network hospitals in your city, pre-authorization process, document checklist, what the hospital will ask for at admission

State-specific: Maharashtra's MJPJAY has different procedures than Tamil Nadu's CMCHIS — the platform handles all 20+ schemes

Function 3 — Enrollment Walkthrough

Step-by-step guide to Ayushman Card via CSC / bank branch / online portal mera.pmjay.gov.in

PMSBY enrollment is a single bank form — automated form-fill and bank-submission guide

Document kit: exactly what to bring, in what format, for each scheme

Function 4 — Claim Support & Unclaimed Recovery

Hospital refuses to honor government scheme: escalation path with scripts and IRDAI contacts

Deceased family member's PMJJBY claim never filed: nominee filing guide + success fee on recovery

Lapsed PMSBY policies with unclaimed amounts: recovery navigation

Ethical design note: the eligibility check must always be free. Fees apply only to assisted enrollment (convenience, not entitlement) and claim recovery (success-fee, not upfront). The family in a Tier 3 town must never pay to learn their rights.

Competitive Moat

Scheme database moat: real-time tracking of 20+ central + state scheme changes. This is operationally difficult — nobody has built it cleanly for consumers. A 12-month head start is defensible.

Vernacular trust brand: 'SurakshaSaathi' in regional language contexts will be the default recommendation in WhatsApp family groups within 2 years if the product works.

Government alignment: 'Your Money, Your Right' initiative validates this problem at the highest political level. Partnership with state health agencies is a real opportunity.

Monetizing vulnerable users requires ethical guardrails: the free eligibility check is non-negotiable. Revenue must come from adjacent services.

Government can build this: they're trying (Jan Suraksha portal, ABDM). Near-term risk is low given execution pace, but a 3–5 year horizon risk exists.

Idea 4  Family Insurance OS

"Your policies are scattered across 6 places. We build the single source of truth."

The average Indian middle-class family has 4–7 insurance policies across multiple insurers — a HDFC Life policy bought through an agent, a Star Health floater from an online aggregator, ICICI Lombard car insurance, a parent's LIC endowment, a PMJJBY enrollment through the bank. None of these talk to each other. There is no single view of the family's protection status.

Bima Sugam will allow digital policy storage — but as a passive repository, not an active management layer. SurakshaSaathi as the 'active family insurance CFO' is structurally differentiated.

Coverage gap map: visual heat-map of family protection status — who is covered for what, where the holes are

Policy vault: secure storage for all policy documents, accessible during hospitalization from any device

Pull integration: Bima Sugam API pull once available; manual PDF upload as Day 1 fallback

Free tier → paid tier: free gives the dashboard; paid (₹399/year) gives advisory, claim support, and broker-assisted gap fill

Idea 5  Vernacular Tier 2/3 Portal

"Bima Sugam solves the backend. Nobody built the human-first frontend."

The Problem

Only 23% of Indian adults demonstrate basic understanding of insurance products. In rural and semi-urban areas, insurance literacy drops below 15%. Policybazaar and Coverfox are built for digitally fluent, English-comfortable users in the top 50 cities. Bima Sugam's Phase 1 rollout is unlikely to solve the UX problem for first-time buyers.

The Tier 2/3 insurance buyer does not want to compare plans. They want to understand what insurance means, why they need it, and what will happen when something goes wrong. That is an education-first, human-assisted journey — not a comparison widget.

Below 15%

Insurer CAC at Tier 2/3

High — opportunity for better rev-share

Hindi + 4 vernacular languages TAM

700M+ potential users

Mobile penetration (Tier 2/3)

Growing rapidly, UPI as baseline digital behavior

The Product

Education-first flow: 'What is health insurance?' → 'Why do I need it?' → 'What will this cover for my family?' — in the user's language, with voice support

AI-assisted explanation: complex policy terms translated into simple vernacular language examples ('Your waiting period means if you get diabetes today, the policy won't pay for diabetes treatment for the next 3 years')

Video-call-to-advisor fallback: when the user reaches a decision point and wants a human, one-click video call to a bilingual advisor. Not a call center — a trained insurance advisor in the same language.

WhatsApp-first: the entire journey available on WhatsApp for users who don't install apps. Policy documents delivered, premium reminders sent, claims tracked — all via WhatsApp.

Phased buy: not 'buy now' as the CTA — 'learn more', then 'talk to advisor', then 'buy'. Reduces drop-off for first-time buyers.

Languages at launch: Hindi, Kannada, Tamil, Telugu, Bengali. Marathi, Gujarati, Malayalam in Phase 2.

IRDAI Bima Vahak alignment: IRDAI's own women-led last-mile distribution initiative (Bima Vahak) is a potential partnership and regulatory tailwind.

Slower revenue: education-first means longer sales cycle. 3–4x more touchpoints before policy purchase vs. Policybazaar.

"62 million businesses. 97% uninsured. Zero quality advisors."

The MSME is too small for a corporate broker (Marsh, Aon only serve large corporates) and too complex for a Policybazaar comparison widget (which can't handle sector-specific risk profiles). Nobody serves the ₹1–10 crore revenue business owner.

Idea 7  Senior Citizen Protection Portal

"India's elderly are being systematically mis-sold. Their adult children are the buyer."

The Problem

Senior citizens are the most systematically targeted and most vulnerable segment in Indian insurance. Bank relationship managers sell ULIPs as 'special FDs' to elderly customers who visit branches to renew fixed deposits. They sign proposal forms without understanding they're buying market-linked products with 5-year lock-in periods. Banks earn up to 65% of the first-year premium as commission on traditional plans — a structural incentive misalignment.

Bank commission on ULIP (Year 1)

Up to 65% of first-year premium

Bank commission on mutual fund

~1% annually — no upfront

Senior citizens auto-entitled to PM-JAY

All 70+ since October 2024

Health insurance premium cap for seniors

10% annual increase cap (IRDAI Jan 2025)

Primary mis-selling victim

Senior citizens — cognitive decline, trust in banks

Buyer persona (digital)

Adult child, 35–55yr, urban, concerned about parents

Crucially: the buyer and the user are different people. The adult child in Bengaluru is digitally fluent and willing to pay to protect their parents in a Tier 2 town. This is the acquisition angle that no portal has exploited.

The Product

Policy Scanner for Seniors

Upload parent's existing policies: detect ULIPs sold as FDs, identify whether the senior actually understood what they were signing, flag mis-selling signals

Output: 'Your father holds 3 ULIPs that were likely mis-sold. Total premiums paid: ₹4.8L. Here are your options: file a mis-selling complaint, surrender (and we'll tell you the penalty), or restructure.'

Entitlement Check

Automatic PM-JAY eligibility check for the senior (all 70+ qualify since Oct 2024)

Senior-specific state scheme check (Kerala Karunya KASP, Maharashtra MJPJAY, etc.)

PMJJBY and PMSBY enrollment status and claim history

Family Caretaker Dashboard

Adult child gets visibility into all parent's policies: renewal dates, nominee status, claim history, premium amounts

Alert system: 'Your mother's health insurance renews in 30 days. We noticed her coverage hasn't been increased in 3 years while medical inflation ran at 13%.

Emergency access: in hospitalization emergency, adult child can access all policy details from the dashboard in one click

Mis-selling Recovery

If ULIP mis-selling is confirmed: guided complaint filing with insurer grievance cell and IRDAI

Success fee on recovered premiums. IRDAI mandates full refund in proven mis-selling cases.

Revenue Model

Family subscription

₹999/year — adult child pays to protect parents

Idea 8  Life Insurance Mis-selling Recovery

"₹40,000 crore locked in wrong policies. IRDAI has named it an alarm."

The Problem

IRDAI has explicitly flagged mis-selling as an 'alarming' level concern in its public communications. Banks distributed 33.1% of new individual life insurance premiums in FY24 — up from 15.6% a decade ago — primarily through ULIPs. The commission structure (up to 65% first-year on traditional plans vs. ~1% annually on mutual funds) has created the largest systematic financial mis-selling problem in Indian retail finance history.

This idea is closest to the Claims Advocacy Portal (Idea 1) in structure but targets a completely different regulatory track — life insurance instead of health insurance — and is entirely untouched by Insurance Samadhan's current playbook which focuses almost exclusively on health claims.

Platform Strategy — How the 8 Ideas Work Together

SurakshaSaathi is not 8 separate products. It is one platform with a unified brand promise — 'your protection companion' — expressed across different life stages, income segments, and insurance categories.

The Funnel Architecture

Each idea feeds the others:

Government Scheme Navigator (Idea 3) is the widest top of funnel — free eligibility checks bring in low-income and senior families who would never pay upfront. Once enrolled in PM-JAY, the natural next step is a top-up policy (Idea 5 or 4).

Vernacular Portal (Idea 5) acquires first-time buyers in Tier 2/3. When their claim gets rejected (as 11% of health claims are), they become Claims Advocacy Portal (Idea 1) users.

Senior Citizen Portal (Idea 7) and Life Mis-selling Recovery (Idea 8) share infrastructure — the same policy audit engine, the same complaint filing system, the same success-fee model.

Policy Health Score (Idea 2) and Family Insurance OS (Idea 4) are complementary — the OS is the wallet, the Score is the advisory layer on top. They can be one product with two views.

MSME Navigator (Idea 6) is intentionally separate from the consumer brand — MSME insurance is B2B sales, not B2C trust-building. Consider a separate sub-brand or product line.
